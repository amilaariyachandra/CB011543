Sri Lankan Turtle Population


Sri Lanka is home to multiple species of turtles as of September 2021,including the critically endangered Hawksbill turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata) and the endangered Green turtle (Chelonia mydas).The populations of these species confront numerous hazards,including habitat loss,pollution, climate change, and poaching.Sri Lanka's turtle population has been under considerable duress for many years.Nevertheless,numerous conservation efforts have been implemented to safeguard these species and their nesting grounds.Several organisations,such as the Turtle Conservation Project (TCP).conduct research, administer conservation programmes, and raise community awareness to conserve turtle populations in Sri Lanka.Rekawa Beach,which is part of the Rekawa Turtle Conservation Project,is a notable birthing location for turtles in Sri Lanka.This shoreline is recognised as one of the nation's most significant nesting grounds for turtles.


Sri Lankan Turtle Hatcheries

Sri Lanka is known for its beautiful coastline and diverse marine life, including several species of sea turtles that inhabit its shores. In order to protect these endangered turtles and ensure their survival, turtle hatcheries have been established along the Sri Lankan coast. These hatcheries play a crucial role in conserving turtle populations and educating the public about the importance of turtle conservation. Turtle hatcheries in Sri Lanka serve as safe havens for sea turtles, providing a protected environment for nesting and hatching. The primary objective of these hatcheries is to collect sea turtle eggs from vulnerable nesting sites and relocate them to secure hatcheries. This prevents the eggs from being poached or destroyed by predators and increases the chances of successful hatching. Once the eggs are relocated to the hatcheries, they are carefully monitored and protected until they hatch. The hatcheries create an ideal environment that mimics the natural conditions required for successful incubation. This includes maintaining proper temperature and humidity levels, as well as providing protection from predators. When the baby turtles hatch, they are given some time to rest and gain strength before being released into the ocean. Hatchery staff and volunteers carefully handle the turtles during this process, ensuring their safety and minimizing any potential stress. Before release, the turtles are typically marked for identification purposes, such as with small tags or notches on their shells. In addition to their primary role in protecting turtle eggs and facilitating successful hatching, Sri Lankan turtle hatcheries also contribute to public awareness and education about sea turtle conservation. Many hatcheries offer guided tours and educational programs where visitors can learn about the life cycle of sea turtles, the threats they face, and the efforts being made to protect them. These activities help raise awareness among locals and tourists alike and foster a sense of responsibility towards the conservation of these magnificent creatures. Overall, the establishment of turtle hatcheries in Sri Lanka has significantly contributed to the preservation of sea turtles and their habitats. Through their conservation efforts and educational initiatives, these hatcheries play a crucial role in ensuring the long-term survival of Sri Lanka's sea turtle populations while promoting a greater understanding of the importance of marine conservation.

The process of operating a turtle hatchery typically involves the following steps:

  • Nesting Site Monitoring: Hatcheries usually have trained personnel who monitor the nesting sites along the coastline. They identify turtle nests, record data about the nesting activity, and assess the vulnerability of the eggs in the natural environment.

  • Egg Collection: Once a nest is identified as vulnerable or threatened, the eggs are carefully collected from the nesting site. It is crucial to handle the eggs with care and ensure they remain in their original position and orientation to maintain the viability of the embryos.

  • Incubation: The collected eggs are transported to the hatchery, where they are placed in specially designed incubation chambers or nests. These chambers mimic the natural nest conditions, including temperature and humidity, to promote successful development.

  • Nesting Area Protection: To prevent predation and disturbance, the nesting area is often marked and protected. This can involve the use of nets, barriers, or signs to deter human activity or animal interference.

  • Egg Relocation: The collected eggs are transported to the hatchery where they will be incubated in a controlled environment. The relocation is done with utmost care to maintain the original nesting position and avoid any damage to the eggs.

    • Turtle hatcheries play a vital role in conserving turtle populations by increasing the survival rate of hatchlings, which face numerous risks during their journey from the nest to the ocean. In some cases, hatcheries also conduct research, educational programs, and public awareness campaigns to promote turtle conservation and raise awareness about the importance of protecting these fascinating creatures and their habitats.
